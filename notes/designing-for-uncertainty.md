The hardest UX problem in AI products isn't the interface. It's helping people calibrate when to act on the output and when to question it.

Most AI tools are designed to look confident. Clean responses, no hedging, no visible uncertainty. This is a deliberate choice — uncertainty looks like failure. But it creates a worse problem: users either over-trust and stop verifying, or a single bad output destroys their confidence entirely.

> "Uncertainty isn't a bug to hide. It's a signal worth designing for."

The better path is to surface confidence honestly. Not with lengthy disclaimers, but with small, readable signals — a source citation, a "based on X" qualifier, an easy override. Users develop calibration through feedback. If the interface never shows them where the AI is shaky, they can't learn to read it.

Tools that do this well treat uncertainty as a design primitive, not an afterthought. It's worth building from the start — retrofitting it later is much harder than it sounds.
